Things to Do

You already know the classic spots (Tour Eiffel & co). These are ideas for discovering another side of the city, district by district.

17th arrondissement

Batignolles

A residential, young, and fairly upscale neighbourhood. You come here if you want authentic Parisian life. It is also a great starting point for exploring Montmartre, the 9th, and the 8th.

Enjoy the Parisian shopping streets (Rue Legendre, Rue Lévis, Rue Cardinet), trendy coffee shops, and good restaurants. The area to cover runs roughly from Pont Cardinet station to Boulevard des Batignolles, and from Avenue de Clichy to Rue Lévis and Parc Monceau.

Local tip: La Fondation hotel has a rooftop with one of the best views in town. This is also the neighbourhood where we have the most places to recommend.
18th arrondissement

Montmartre

Usually packed with tourists, which can be annoying if done badly. Our favourite time to visit Montmartre is at night, when the Sacré-Coeur is illuminated. Have dinner at Chez Pradel or drinks at Mikkeller, and enjoy the walk back to see Montmartre in a completely different light.

If you go during the day, escape the crowds and head to the Hôtel Particulier for tea or a cocktail in the garden. It is a hidden hotel that will make you feel like you are in the countryside in seconds.

Wander the streets, visit the Montmartre Museum and the vineyards. For fabric lovers, check out the shops near Marché Saint-Pierre. Walk down Rue des Martyrs for plenty of shops and food stops, and you will quickly arrive in the 9th, a more residential district with fewer tourist traps.

If you spend the evening: Check out Pigalle and catch a glimpse of the Moulin Rouge.
9th arrondissement

Pigalle & the 9th

Intertwined with Montmartre but less touristic and with better quality options. Packed with great bars and restaurants along Rue Condorcet, Rue des Martyrs, Rue de Douai, Rue Blanche, and Rue Lafayette.

An easy spot to transition to shopping in the 8th (Le Printemps, Galeries Lafayette, and for the food lovers, Lafayette Gourmet), or head towards the 10th to keep exploring trendy shops, restaurants, and bars.

1st & 2nd arrondissements

Opéra & Tuileries

Start with the Opéra Garnier, which is magnificent from the inside. Even better if you manage to get tickets for a show. Then discover Rue Sainte-Anne, the Japanese street of Paris, with countless ramen options (our favourite being Sanjo).

Walk through the Palais Royal gardens, and you will find the Louvre, the Musée d'Orsay, and the Tuileries all within reach.

The very Parisian thing to do: Grab a drink and sit on the Quai de Seine at golden hour. Guaranteed to make you fall in love with the city.
2nd, 3rd & 4th arrondissements

Sentier & Le Marais

Very touristic, but here you cannot really avoid it. Walk down Rue Montorgueil to see how the French are obsessed with food. Take the old streets to enjoy the architecture (Rue Tiquetonne), and explore the covered passages with their wonderfully random shops.

Keep wandering towards Arts et Métiers to reach Le Marais, where you can get lost in the old Paris streets. If you are tired of the crowds, head up to the Haut Marais (towards République and Canal Saint-Martin). If you love the energy, go down towards the Seine, and perhaps even to the Île de la Cité to see Notre-Dame.

10th & 11th arrondissements

République, Canal Saint-Martin & Canal de l'Ourcq

The other very attractive, young, residential district of Paris. You are in the east of the city, which is trendier and more alternative. The offer of cool spots here is overwhelming, and the only thing to do is enjoy it.

Some ideas: walk along Canal Saint-Martin, visit Parc des Buttes-Chaumont, explore the streets near Oberkampf, and walk the full length of Rue Saint-Maur. It can get sketchy in some spots: if you feel uncomfortable, change street. The closer you go to central Paris, the better it gets.

Extras: Unforgettable nightlife. The Marché de Bastille where you can eat oysters on the spot. Rent a boat on Canal de l'Ourcq.
5th, 6th & 7th arrondissements

Rive Gauche

The other side of the Seine has plenty to offer. We know it less as it is further from where we live and where our friends are, but there are some wonderful spots worth crossing the river for.

Highlights
Jardin du Luxembourg, Jardin des Plantes (with its zoo, and very close to the Grande Mosquée for mint tea), the Panthéon, Le Bon Marché and La Grande Épicerie de Paris (a food lover's paradise), Musée Rodin, and the charming neighbourhood of Butte-aux-Cailles.

Extra Ideas

Museums
Fondation Louis Vuitton (stunning Frank Gehry building in the Bois de Boulogne), Musée d'Orsay (Impressionist masterpieces in a former train station), Musée du Quai Branly (world cultures, right by the Eiffel Tower).
Activities
Crazy Horse (cabaret show, very classy), Annette K (swimming pool floating on the Seine).

Where to Stay

Our advice on the best neighbourhoods to book your accommodation, and a few to avoid.

We advise staying on what is called the Rive Droite (right bank): when you look at a map of Paris, that is the part above the Seine. This is where most of our favourite spots are, and where you will be closest to us and to the wedding venue.

A quick note on how Paris works: districts (quartiers) are often named after the closest metro station. There are several districts per arrondissement (20 arrondissements in total), and a single district can overlap several arrondissements.
